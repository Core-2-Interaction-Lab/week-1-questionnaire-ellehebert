Keep in mind some may view it as a form of artistic expression or cultural resistance, others see it as a light on public spaces that can contribute to a sense of disorder and crime. History of Graffiti in New York City

1966-71

NYC graffiti was initially used by activists and gangs, but its current identity formed in the late 1960s. Graffiti's history begins in Philadelphia in the mid to late 1960s, where writers like CORNBREAD and TITY gained notoriety by tagging their names all over the city. Although the Philadelphia movement predates NYC's, there's no direct connection between the two. In NYC, the movement started independently around 1967 with JULIO 204 in the northernmost part of Manhattan. He gained attention by writing his name on streets and subway stations in his neighborhood before retiring after a short period.

1971-74

Following CORNBREAD and JULIO, Washington Heights gave birth to writers like TAKI, PHIL T GREEK, GREG 69, and others. TAKI, a foot messenger, gained notoriety for his prolific tagging on subways and streets, and a 1971 New York Times article on "TAKI 183 Spawns Penpals" sparked public curiosity. Meanwhile, Brooklyn also saw a growing movement with early writers like FRIENDLY FREDDIE, UNDERTAKER ASH, and APP SUPER HOG. Writing moved from streets to subways, becoming a unifying element for the separate movements and establishing inter-borough competition. Writers discovered the concept and method of "bombing" by hitting many subway cars in train yards or lay-ups with less chance of getting caught.

Tag Style

With so many writers, fame was no longer just about volume, so they started developing unique tags and calligraphic styles with flourishes, stars, and other designs to distinguish themselves. Crowns were used by writers who declared themselves king..

1975-1977

Innovation in writing hit a plateau after 1974 and a new generation of writers began to reap the benefits of prior artistic foundations and a fiscally challenged New York City transit system. This led to heavy bombing, where throw-ups became the definitive form of bombing. Crews like POG, 3YB, BYB, TC, and TOP made major contributions, and throw-up kings included TEE, IZ, DY 167, PI, IN, LE, TO, OI, FI aka VINNY, TI 149, CY, and PEO. The trend peaked from '75 through '77, as did whole cars, and writers like BUTCH, CASE, KINDO, BLADE, COMET, ALE 1, DOO2, JOHN 150, KIT 17, MARK 198, LEE, MONO, SLAVE, SLUG, DOC 109, and CAINE ONE plastered the IRTs with magnificent whole cars.

1978-1981

Graffiti art reached its peak in the late 1970s with crews like TDS, TMT, UA, CIA, and TF5. This was the last major wave of graffiti before the Transit Authority made the elimination of graffiti a priority. The graffiti removal program, known as The Buff, started up again in 1980 and many writers started to quit and consider other creative options. However, established galleries like ESSES, Fashion Moda, and Fun Gallery proved to be important in expanding graffiti overseas. European art dealers became aware of the movement and were receptive to the new art form. Exhibitions featuring paintings by DONDI, LEE, ZEPHYR, LADY PINK, DAZE, FUTURA 2000 and others exposed the world to the once secret world of New York's youth.

1982-1985

In the 1980s, the writing culture declined due to the crack cocaine epidemic, firearms, new laws, and the MTA's anti-graffiti budget increase. The MTA made favored painting areas inaccessible and graffiti removal more consistent. This frustrated some writers, while others became more aggressive and territorial, leading to cross-out wars and conflicts in yards and lay-ups. Some writers still thrived during this time, including SKEME, DEZ, TRAP, DELTA, SHARP, SEEN TC5, SHY 147, BOE, WEST, KAZE, SPADE 127, SAK, VULCAN, SHAME, BIO, MIN, DURO, KEL, T KID, MACK, NICER, BRIM, BG 183, KENN, CEM, FLIGHT, AIRBORN, RIZE, JON 156, KYLE 156, and the X Men.

1985-1989

In the early to mid-1980s, several factors led to the deterioration of the writing culture, including the crack cocaine epidemic, powerful firearms, and laws restricting the sale of paint to minors. The MTA's anti-graffiti budget increased significantly, leading to tighter security and the removal of graffiti. However, on certain subway lines headed for scrap yards, writers like WANE, WEN, DERO, and TKID took advantage of a last opportunity to hit white 5s with burners. Poor artistic efforts were also made due to a lack of paint and courage to stay in a lay-up for prolonged periods of time, and the violence subsided by mid-1986. Only a handful of diehard writers, such as GHOST, SENTO, and KET, remained prominent figures to keep transit writing alive.

1989- PRESENT

On May 12, 1989 the MTA declared a victory over graffiti. The MTA set in effect a policy of removing all marked subway cars from service. The objective being no graffiti will run. This was the birth of what is known as the Clean Train movement. There are many writers who believe subway painting is the defining act in being a writer. Walls, freights, scraps, and canvas are for fake writers. These writers refuse to give up the battle against the MTA. Even though works do not run or only run for one trip many people still write.
